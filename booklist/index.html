I've worked as both a developer and manager developer. These are the books that I've found very helpful in helping me become a better developer.

Technical Books

The Mythical Man-Month, Anniversary Edition: Essays On Software Engineering (2nd Edition)

This is one of the classics of our field. There is a lot of great info in here, including the first debunking of the waterfall method. Just read it.

The Pragmatic Programmer: From Journeyman to Master

Much like the Mythical Man Month, this is a classic and a must read. Instead of teaching you to code, it offers a philosphy about how to be a good developer and how to approach building your applications.

Code Complete (2nd Edition) (Developer Best Practices)

This book, in print, is large and intimidating. So much so that it took me several years to work through the entire book. But it's the first book that made me think about having design rules for the code that I write. One of the key points that supports this is the idea that you should consistently ordering your function arguments. It's a great lesson that I never thought of before. I also really enjoy the pseudo-code method of pre-coding. I still do that year after reading this book.

Architectural Styles and the Design of Network-based Software Architectures

This is Roy E Fielding's dissertation that describes REST. Too many people in our industry don't really understand REST. Don't be one of those people and read this. It's short. And don't let the word "dissertation" scare you away.

Agile Software Development with Scrum (Series in Agile Software Development)

I call this the little black scrum book. It's the first book I read about agile development and it has stuck with me. While it does provide a good outline in SCRUM, it also promotes that being agile is more than a standardized process. It's teaches the fundamental of incrementally improving both your software and your process for building software.

Developer Evangalism Handbook

This outlines how you should think and talk about your code, whether it's in front of large audiences or amongst your own co-workers. I routinely come back to this to get me in a mindset to setup for a public speaking engagement.

JavaScript: The Good Parts

This is the only language book that I include on this list. I love Javascript, but that's not the reason I included it. This provides some great things to think about when thinking about programming languages. This was the first time I thought to start looking at the features of the language that I find most useful. It's also one of the first times I really thought about following a strict coding style that supports the best parts of a language. Crockford also provides a great visual way to describe a programming language that I haven't seen in any other programming book that I've read.

The Art of UNIX Programming

I just bought this book. It's one that I've always wanted. So far I've only gotten through the chapter on the history. That alone makes this book worth the price. I highly suggest buying it.

The Phoenix Project: A Novel About IT, DevOps, and Helping Your Business Win

I haven't actually read this yet, but it comes highly recommended. I've heard that it's a must-read for anyone intersted in dev-ops. All developers should be interested in dev-ops.

Becoming a Better Programmer: A Handbook for People Who Care About Code

This has a very similar style to Pragmatic Programmer. It really makes you think about what it means to be a good developer. It offers a lot of great one-liners that you can build into your daily thought process. My favorite is a play on a Boy Scout Rule. "Whenever you touch some code leave it better than you found it."

Non-Technical Books

Lead the Field

This is a great book for anyone striving to reach the top 5% of their profession. I listen to this audiobook every few months while waiting for Audible credits. We are all full of diamonds waiting to be mined. It's both motivational and enlightening.

3-d Negotiation: Powerful Tools to Change the Game in Your Most Important Deals

If you've been a developer for some time, you've had to negotiate. It could have been with a team member about implementation. Or about getting time to eliminate technical debt from your code base. Or about something else altogether. This book offers some great tips to help you with your negotiating skills.

Crucial Conversations Tools for Talking When Stakes Are High, Second Edition

This is another audiobook that I listen to often. As a developer, you will run into a lot of crucial conversations. This can happen with superiors, subordinates, other developers, and with clients. It's important to know how to identify when crucial conversations are happening and how to best answer those.

The Effective Executive: The Definitive Guide to Getting the Right Things Done (Harperbusiness Essentials)

This book isn't about old men in suits. It's about what all of us programmers are -- knowledge workers. This is a great read for anyone that works in the software field, from programmer to designer to manager. We can all benefit from the things that Drucker talks about in this book.

The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin

It's very interesting reading about the journey of an early american entrepreneur in a time long before the internet. He offers some great thoughts on personal productivity and wotk ethic.

Conclusion

I hope you find this book list helpful. If you have any suggestions for books I should add, please send them to me on twitter @shortpgh.

I have on parting tip for you. Buy these book as ebooks that you can access from anywhere -- home, work, or on the road. You'll never know when you might need to re-read a chapter or a lesson from one of these books.

And tip #2, I suggest reading these on a kindle (even if you buy them somewhere else) simple because of the note recording capability. Highlight and note the sections and quotes that you find important. It makes it easy to quickly re-read what you found important in each book. I refer to my notes often. capability.
